7.1.1 How broadly should the definition of research data be defined (e.g. software, process data, etc.)?

7.1.2 Should primary data be defined and/or digital and analogue data distinguished?

  • Research data include, among other things, measurement data, laboratory values, audiovisual information, texts, survey data, objects from collections or samples that are created, developed or evaluated in scientific work. Methodological test procedures such as questionnaires, software and simulations can also represent central results of scientific research and should therefore also be included under the term research data.

  • Research data are digital data generated by scientific source research, experiments, measurements, simulations, surveys or surveys. They include structured mass data as well as unstructured data of various formats and contents, including process data such as processing steps, algorithms and programs developed in a research project or digitized fonts, images or films.

7.1.3 How is RDM defined?

Research data management encompasses all measures – beyond research action in the narrower sense also organization-related – that must be taken in order to obtain high-quality data, to comply with good scientific practice in the data life cycle, to make results reproducible and data available for reuse, and to meet any existing documentation obligations […]

7.1.4 What is research?

Research is the search for new insights – as opposed to accidental discovery – as well as their systematic documentation and publication.

7.1.5 How are researchers defined?

Researchers are all members of the [name of institution] […], in particular employees and doctoral candidates. The term also includes persons who are not directly associated with [name of institution] […] but who use the infrastructure of [name of institution] […] for research purposes. Guest researchers or cooperation partners are also expected to comply with the policy.

7.1.6 What should a DMP contain?

Funding organisations are increasingly requiring project participants to draw up a data management plan and scientific institutions to adapt to the requirements of sustainable data management. Data management plans at the beginning of a project or research work are suitable for describing the data to be used and generated and the necessary documentation, metadata and standards, for identifying possible legal restrictions (e.g. data protection) in good time, for planning required storage resources and for determining criteria for which data is to be made available to external parties in what form and how long the data is to be secured. At the organizational level, research institutions (e.g. universities) must ensure access to appropriate infrastructure services within the institution (e.g. by building up and expanding suitable capacities) or in cooperation with external partners (through cooperation agreements, etc.).

7.1.7 What is a repository?

  • A repository is a database or data archive for the storage and publication of digital research data with the primary purpose of keeping it available and citable and reusable for an unlimited period of time. Through appropriate rights and license management, different levels of access to the research data (e.g. project-internal, cross-project and public) can be regulated and their access and usage conditions can be defined. 33

  • A digital repository is a mechanism for managing and storing digital content. Repositories can be subject or institutional in their focus. Putting content into an institutional repository enables staff and institutions to manage and preserve it, and therefore derive maximum value from it. […] Repositories use open standards to ensure that the content they contain is accessible in that it can be searched and retrieved for later use. The use of these agreed international standards allows mechanisms to be set up which import, export, identify, store and retrieve the digital content within the repository. Digital repositories may include a wide range of content for a variety of purposes and users. […] Typically content can include research outputs such as journal articles or research data, e-theses, e-learning objects and teaching materials, and administrative data.34

7.1.8 What is metadata?

Metadata refers to all additional information that is necessary or useful for the interpretation of the actual data, e.g. research data, and that enables (automatic) processing of the research data by technical systems. Metadata is therefore often referred to as ’data about data’ and serves to categorize and characterize the different information about digital objects: Technical metadata contains, for example, information on data volume and data format and is of central importance for sustainable data storage. Descriptive metadata (also known as descriptive or content metadata) provides information about the information contained in digital objects (e.g. scientific) and thus decides on its find- ability, referencing and reusability. In order for measurement data, for example, to be interpretable and reusable, the complete and correct indication of the […] Unity is essen- tial. Descriptive explanations (e.g. in the form of an abstract), which are kept together with the (research) data, are also useful. This also includes references to rights of use, equipment used, standards used if no corresponding publication is available.35

7.1.9 What are persistent identifiers?

Persistent identifiers are used for the clear and permanent addressing of digital re- sources. Unlike traditional web URLs, they distinguish between identification and address of a resource. With the help of a resoluting mechanism, it can be ensured that a resource can still be accessed even if its physical storage location has changed.
